# Executive Summary — quality calibration examples

Patterns from real SOWs approved by Google for DAF/PSF funding. Match this
level of specificity, professionalism, and rigor when producing the
`executive_summary` field.

## Executive Summary Patterns

### Good: Business-first, technical-second, outcome-driven
> "Banco BTG Pactual is accelerating its product delivery capabilities by adopting
> an advanced, AI-driven agent designed to support Product Managers throughout the
> entire product lifecycle — from discovery to backlog refinement and delivery
> tracking."

**Why it works**: Customer's strategic initiative first, specific capability named,
lifecycle scope defined in one sentence.

### Good: Scope boundary in the summary itself
> "This engagement is strictly limited to the assessment and definition phase.
> No development, deployment, or configuration of agents is included in this
> Statement of Work."

**Why it works**: Reviewer knows exactly what this SOW covers and doesn't before
reading details.


### Good: Template-compliant, business-value rich, technical outcomes clear
> "This Statement of Work (SOW) outlines the scope, activities, deliverables, and estimated timelines for the design and implementation of a reusable cloud-based platform that enables [Customer] to modernize a strategically important business capability, standardize delivery patterns, and accelerate future workload adoption on Google Cloud.
>
> The engagement supports [Customer]'s objective of moving from isolated implementations toward a governed operating model where business capabilities can be delivered through repeatable architecture, controlled integrations, secure data handling, and measurable technical outcomes. The expected business value includes faster delivery of new use cases, reduced operational fragmentation, improved governance and traceability, and a clearer foundation for scaling future initiatives.
>
> The technical outcomes include the target architecture, implementation of the approved Google Cloud services, integration with the identified enterprise systems, delivery of the agreed workstreams, testing and validation of the core solution components, and handover of the documented operating model to [Customer]'s technical stakeholders. The scope also includes the approved pilot or initial workload when defined in the SOW, allowing the engagement to validate the platform or solution pattern against a concrete business scenario.
>
> The delivery approach is structured around the agreed timeline and workstreams, including architecture refinement, implementation, integration, testing, documentation, knowledge transfer, and acceptance support. This engagement is strictly limited to the scope defined in this SOW and excludes unsupported integrations, unrelated workloads, ongoing production operations, and service-level guarantees unless explicitly included through a formally approved Change Request.
>
> This scope of work will be funded with Google [Deal Acceleration Funds (DAF) | Google Partner Services Funds (PSF)]."

**Why it works**: It follows the required SOW template wording, explains business value before technical scope, names high-level technical outcomes, includes delivery shape and scope boundaries, and ends with the required Google funding sentence. It is generic enough to calibrate platform, implementation, migration, foundation, and multi-phase engagements without being tied to a specific customer or project.
